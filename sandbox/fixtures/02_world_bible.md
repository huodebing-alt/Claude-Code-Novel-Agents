# World Bible — *The Trial of Memory*

## Scope brief

A near-future Earth (year 2061), single republic referenced only as "the Republic." The world bible is intentionally minimal — the speculative element is one law, one institution, and its consequences for the few hundred people who serve it. Geography is a city; politics is a court; the magic system is a memory-extraction protocol called Mneme.

## Geography

The novel takes place entirely in one unnamed coastal capital, in three places:

- **The Reader College.** A walled compound in the old quarter, formerly a Carthusian monastery. Cloisters, a garden, a library of glass-cased memory records. Readers live here from their induction at seventeen until their forced retirement at forty-five.
- **The Court of Mneme.** A circular hall, no public seating, no press. A central well of dim water (the *speculum*) into which Readers descend the extracted records and re-render them as testimony.
- **The Eastern Quarter.** Working-class district across the canal. Used to be served by tram; the line was decommissioned twenty years ago after the Mneme inquiries. This is where Lin Wei's mother lived; this is where Lin Wei was born.

Climate: cold, damp, the gulls audible from the College garden in winter.

## Politics

Single republic, parliamentary. The Court of Mneme is constitutionally independent of the legislature. The Reader Council (a body of nine) appoints and disciplines all sitting Readers. Council seats are coveted — they confer immunity from the cross-case contamination inquiry that ends most Readers' careers in disgrace.

Tensions:

- Reformers in the legislature have introduced three bills in five years to abolish Mneme evidence; all have failed.
- An underground memory-trade exists. Unauthorized extractions for grief, for closure, for confession. Punishable by twenty years.
- Public mistrust of Readers ("mind-eaters") grows after each scandal; the College has stopped recruiting in the eastern quarter.

## Magic / Tech: The Mneme Protocol

### One-line

Memory rendered admissible by neural extraction. Trained Reader-jurists, born synesthete, translate the extracted record from sensory shorthand (color, texture, sound) into legal testimony.

### Rules

1. **Window.** Memory can be extracted from any consenting adult within 72 hours of the event with high fidelity. Older memory degrades; >72 hours yields ~30% fidelity and is inadmissible.
2. **Chain of custody.** Extraction must be witnessed by two parties: a recording technician and a court officer. Self-extraction is illegal — no chain.
3. **Reader access.** Only certified Readers (synesthete jurists) may interpret the record. Records cannot be transcribed into text without a Reader; the data is not natively linguistic.
4. **Court submission.** A Reader renders the memory in open court, before the *speculum*. Their interpretation is the testimony.

### Costs

- **For donors:** The extracted window leaves the donor amnesiac for that window. Most donate fragments; a few — usually those confessing — donate hours or days.
- **For Readers:** Reading another person's memory leaves a faint contamination trace. Over a career, Readers accumulate cross-case memory bleed — fragments of other people's lives surface in their own. Forced retirement at forty-five exists to prevent the bleed from compounding.
- **For the law:** Mneme convictions are nearly impossible to overturn. The reformers argue this is the cost.

### Limits

- Memory of a person who has died cannot be extracted (their tissue degrades within minutes; the technical reason is unimportant to the novel).
- Memory of a memory (someone remembering a previous extraction) is unreliable and rejected.
- A Reader cannot read their own memory.
- A Reader cannot read the memory of a blood relative — conflict of interest, but also, the contamination is too strong.

### Cultural impact

- Synesthete children are flagged at age seven by the standard education panel; flagged children are eligible for Reader training, with consent of guardian. Many guardians refuse; many cannot.
- Readers are cloistered. They do not marry, do not have civilian friendships, do not appear on entertainment programs.
- Outside the College, public physical contact with a sitting Reader is avoided. Their gloves are not law but custom.

## Culture

### Defining taboo: Reader-touch

A civilian does not initiate physical contact with a sitting Reader. A Reader's bare hand is gloved in public. Touch during an active case can be argued in court as memory tampering and has, twice, overturned convictions. Lin Wei brushes her father's hand in chapter 3; the gesture is itself a question.

### Defining ceremony: Retirement gowning

At a Reader's forty-fifth birthday, their robes are ceremonially burned at dawn in the College garden. The retiring Reader is led, barefoot, from the cloister by her named successor — usually the Reader she once mentored. The novel opens with Lin Wei in the garden, watching her mentor walk.

### Other notes

- **Food:** the College serves a single rotating menu of broths, root vegetables, and a sweet rice porridge. Readers do not eat strongly flavored food during active cases; sensory load.
- **Dress:** Readers wear unbleached linen under their robes. The robes are a deep oxblood, fading to rust at the cuffs over a career.
- **Religion:** the Republic is secular; the College has retained pre-secular monastic forms (vigils, the dawn bell, vespers without theology). Most Readers do not call them religious; some quietly do.
- **Calendar:** standard solar. Court does not sit on the four equinox/solstice days.

## What is *not* in this bible

No technology beyond Mneme. No global politics. No economic system distinct from a recognizable contemporary mixed economy. No religion with theology. No alien anything. The world is unobtrusive because the *interior* is the planet we are visiting.
